Internal Communication

Internal communication should aim to embed desired safety behaviors by reinforcing positive practices, recognizing safe actions, and providing constructive feedback for improvement. Engaging staff in risk management involves encouraging active participation in hazard identification and risk assessment, soliciting input on safety procedures, and empowering them to take ownership of safety in their work areas. Enhancing transparency and accountability requires keeping staff informed about safety policies, procedures, and performance, clearly defining roles and responsibilities, and holding individuals accountable for their safety performance.

Examples:

  • Publicly acknowledging staff who consistently follow infection control protocols.
  • Holding brainstorming sessions for hazard identification in clinical areas.
  • Sharing incident reports (with patient confidentiality maintained) and discussing preventative measures.

Risk Communication

Risk communication is vital for promoting understanding and collaboration. Discussing hazards and risk control processes involves explaining the rationale behind safety procedures, using clear language, and providing opportunities for questions. Clearly communicating the potential consequences of hazards, describing implemented controls, and explaining their protective function is essential. Regular updates on safety performance, use of various communication channels, and timely communication during changes are crucial. Active listening ensures employees feel heard and receive feedback on their concerns.

Examples:

  • Explaining why specific sterilization procedures are necessary to prevent cross-contamination.
  • Holding a Q&A session after implementing a new X-ray safety protocol.
  • Providing regular updates on infection control audit results.
  • When a staff member reports a slippery floor, ensuring the area is marked off, cleaned, and that the reporting staff member is informed of the actions taken.

Team Involvement

Team involvement fosters shared responsibility. Monitoring compliance with safety procedures, addressing deviations promptly, and explaining the rationale behind safety rules are essential. Creating a culture where staff feel comfortable providing feedback and actively soliciting input improves safety practices. Providing access to relevant safety information, clear instructions, accessible manuals, and documented policies ensures staff are well-informed. Ensuring all staff understand expectations and know where to seek support completes the framework. By focusing on these strategies, dental practices can create a communication-rich environment that empowers staff to take an active role in maintaining a safe and healthy workplace.

Examples:

  • Conducting regular safety audits and providing feedback to staff.
  • Holding team meetings to discuss safety concerns and suggestions.
  • Providing easy access to SDSs and equipment manuals in the staff room.
  • Clearly outlining the procedure for reporting safety hazards in the employee handbook.
  • Designating a specific staff member as a safety champion to provide support and guidance.
